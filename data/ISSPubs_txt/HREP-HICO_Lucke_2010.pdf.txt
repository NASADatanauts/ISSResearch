The Hyperspectral Imager for the Coastal Ocean (HICO) is the only spacebome
hyperspectral sensor designed for characterization of the coastal maritime environment. It
was taken from a set of written requirements to a complete hardware package ready for
spacecraft-level testing in 16 months. It had to meet NASA’s safety requirements for the
ISS. A means of directing the sensor’s line of sight to off—nadir directions was essential. '
Construction of HICO was made possible by extensive use of commercial off—the-shelf
' (COTS) components, with minor modiﬁcations for spaceﬂight/Vacuum conditions where
' necessary. Efficient engineering combined these components into a complete system that _
met all requirements. . ' .
1. Introduction I . '
. The HICO sensor isthe ﬁrst spaceborne hyperspectral imager designed speciﬁcally
. for the coastal environment. HICO builds on the legacy of NRL’s airborne, now space- -
borne, hyperspectral remote sensing program. Extensive experience developing and
operating the PHILLS airborne hyperspectral imagers1 enabled NRL’s Remote Sensing
Division to begin with a set of written requirements for HICO in May, 2007, and deliver
a space-qualiﬁed instrument for integration and spacecraﬁ-level testing in September,

data-processing solutions. - '

2. HICO’s Mission and Sensor ' '
~ , Visible and near infrared wavelengths in the range of 400 to 700 nm constitute the
only portion of the electromagnetic spectrum that penetrates water and directly probes the .
water column. In the complicated coastal environment, where the water contains
signiﬁcant dissolved and suspended matter and the bottom may be detectable,
hyperspectral imaging has demonstrated the ability to retrieve bathymetry, bottom type,
chlorophyll content, and the inherent optical properties of the water5’6. HICO has a GSD
. , of approximately 90 m and is intended to .be a pathfinder for follow-on sensors with
better spatial, and perhaps spectral, resolution that are designed for littoral, riverine, and
estuary-type environments. _ .
Viewed from space, bodies of water are dark, with an albedo of only several percent,
and are seen through the atmosphere which, due to scattered sunlight, is signiﬁcantly
brighter in visible wavelengths than the water. These conditions impose stringent ‘
requirements for maritime hyperspectral imaging which are in general not fully met by
systems designed for land observations7'. These requirements include a minimum 200:1
SNR for water-penetrating wavelengths; full and contiguous spectral coverage at least
over 400 to 700nm, recorded in spectral bands of about 10 nm width or smaller to
resolve the spectral» features in the- scene; and areal coverage of thouSands of square '
kilometers. In addition, spectral image data from 700 to 900 nm or beyond areneeded to
accurately remove light frOm surface reﬂections and the effects of the atmosphere so that
light returned from below the—surface can be measured and analyzed. The HICO sensor
. meets or exceeds these requirements. _ . '
HICO is a conventional hyperspectral imaging sensor, incorporating an Offner .
~ grating-type spectrometer8 and covering a scene in push-broom mode. The optical layout
- is shown in Figure 2. The lens, spectrometer, and camera are mounted on a rigid optical .
bench that is attached by silicon-rubber isolators to a cradle that is rotated about a'
* horizontal axis to direct the LOS to the desired off-nadir direction (see Fig. 1).

3. Mechanical Engineering ' ' '
HICO, along with the rest of HREP, had to meet NASA’S safety requirements for the _
~ 188. These requirements are distinct from considerations that affect only the functioning
"" of the instrument. Thus, for example, parts inside the sealed housing for the camera were -
exempt from safety requirements because, if they failed, thy might well ruin HICO’s
mission, but would not affect the 188 because they are fully contained. A CAD model of
structural components was generated to show that all Stresses were ~well below material
strength and certiﬁed fasteners were used, except for internal, fully contained,
applications. Compliance with these requirements did not constitute an excessive burden
on the program. . ' .

‘ Airborne hyperspectral sensors typically cover an area of interest by ﬂying directly .
' over it, but it is a fundamental problem of a spacebome sensor that the platform Will i
rarely pass directly over a desired ground observation point. It must therefore be possible
to direct the sensor’s LOS over a reasonable cross-track ﬁeld of regard (FOR). This is '
' often done with a steering mirror, Which has the advantage of having a minimal rotating
mass, requiring low-mass supports and drive motor. But changing the reﬂeCtance angle of
a mirror affects the polarization state of the reﬂected light and this is a problem for many
' optical sensors, including HICO. Since minimizing mass was unnecessary for HICO, the
' solution was to rotate the entire optical bench about the direction of ﬂight to direct the ‘
LOS as desired. - - ' - . ' A

4. Computer Control and Data Handling - I
- The COTS software for the Rolera camera runs on a standard PC with the Windows .
OS. A PC 104 processor was installed in HREP to control the HICO and RAIDS sensors.
This is one of many such processors used on the'ISS. Our experience with failures

v 5. Thermal Control ..
The thermal control problem for HICO is eased by its location inside HREP, where it
is well buffered from intra—orbit temperature variations, resulting primarily from passage ‘ .
through, the Earth’s shadow. The most important objects for thermal control are the ' ‘ '
camera and the lens, which must be kept at, or close to, 22° C (room temperature). ~
_ ' Temperature changes will affect the dark current in the camera’s CCD and the focal point
of the lens. v _ - '
' The camera is a PhOtometrics Qimaging Rolera MGi camera, SN Q20431,
incorporating a 512x512 CCD. CCDs normally use a thermo—electric Cooler (TEC) to
' stabilize the pixels’ temperature and assure that dark current is constant and can therefore
be reliably subtracted from the measured data. But the Rolera’s TEC consumes about 80
watts of power,' and, in HICO’s mounting conﬁguration on the spacecraft, providing a
means to dissipate that much heat was beyond the program’s resources. Consequently,
the temperature of the CCD detector is not held conStant and the dark counts change as
temperature changes. Dark counts run around 300, a small, but not negligible, fraction of
a typical observation of a few to several thousand Counts. If the CCD’s initial temperature
~ changes from one scene to the next, the dark counts also change. More troublesome, the
CCD warmsup and cools down during an observation; which makes the dark count rate a
function of time during the collection of data.
HICO follows the normal practice for hyperspectral sensors of measuring dark counts
both before and after the observation of a scene. With these before—and-aﬁer
measurements, it should be possible to reconstruct the dark count rate as a function of
time during the scene observation and subtract it out. Measurements made during thermal
vacuum testing allowed us to develop an empirical model of the time dependence of the
dark counts. As shown in Figure 5, the ﬁrst and last sets of 200 frames are dark data, the
central set of 2,000 frames is from the scene, also dark in this case. There isa 5 minute
- break in data collection after each set. The dark data from the ﬁrst and last sets are used ’
' to predict the dark data from the central set. The ﬁgure shows that this model accurately
describes the time dependence of the dark counts and permits them to be subtracted from
. the measured scene data. ' . .

6. Operations ' . '
HICO executes a normal observation in the following way. BefOre the observation, .
the instrument is in stow position, with the lens looking into the labyrinth light seal (see _
Fig. 1). The seal is intended to establish the dark level for the sensor and to protect the ~
lens, to the extent possible, from contaminants from the 188. First, the motor rotates the
optical bench a few degrees from the stow position and looks for the zero‘position
. indicator from the rotator, to assure that the relation between the commanded motor
position and actual LOS pointing has not been compromised. The motor then returns the .
bench to stow position, camera data collection is initiated, and 200 frames of dark data
are taken. Data collection is stOpped for ﬁve minutes while the motor (slowly!) turns the
LOS to the desired direction for an Earth-surface observation. ' '
, To make an observation at + 45° off—nadir (to port or north), the bench need turn
through only 15°, but must turn through 90° to make an observation at — 30° (to starboard
or south). This motion takes place during the ﬁxed ﬁve-minute interval. Camera data .
collection is re-initiated and 2,000 frames are recorded. The time it takes to collect these
2,000 ﬂames of scene data is 27.5 sec. During a second ﬁve-minute pause, the bench
returns to the stow position after which another 200 frames of dark data are taken. The
entire process takes about 18 minutes. This constraint, combined with the 28 min data
transmission time, limits us to one scene per orbit. ' ' .
As previously noted, there are 15.7 orbits per day. Targets are chosen from a list a ‘
- few days before they are observed. Depending on the relation, between the deSired targets .
and the orbit of the ISS, anywhere from two or three to a dozen or so targets are Covered
in a typical day. » *
Pointing and other commands for tWo to three days of Operation are sent from NRL to ‘
the Payload Operations Integration Center (POIC) at NASA Marshall Space Flight '
Center. From the POIC the commands are sent to the 188 where HICO executes them '
autonomously. The image and ancillary data, e.g., from the HREP star tracker, are stored
in the HREP data system until transfer to the 188. After reception on the ground, the data .
. are sent from NASA to NRL for processing, analysis, distribution, andarchiving. '
